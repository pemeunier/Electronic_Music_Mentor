topic: Chromatic harmony
summary: Chords and techniques that reach beyond the diatonic scale — borrowed chords from parallel modes, secondary dominants that apply V–I pull to non-tonic targets, modulations that change key, augmented sixths and chromatic mediants — all used to add colour and directed motion that diatonic harmony alone cannot supply.
principles:
  - Borrowed chords (modal interchange) draw chords from the parallel mode to colour a progression without leaving the home key — in major, the minor-mode iv, bVI, and bVII are the commonest imports, lending a fleeting minor-mode tinge that resolves back to the tonic.
  - Secondary dominants apply the V–I relationship to a chord other than the tonic — V/V (a dominant of the dominant), V/ii, V/vi and so on — temporarily treating a diatonic chord as a local tonic and intensifying its arrival with a chromatic raised leading tone.
  - Modulation shifts the tonal centre, either smoothly through a pivot chord common to both keys, abruptly by direct modulation, or through a common tone held across the change; the ear accepts the new key once a cadence confirms it.
  - Augmented sixth chords (Italian, French, German) are pre-dominant sonorities built on the lowered sixth degree whose resolution to the dominant (or V/V) is encoded in their voice-leading, a chromatic intensification of the pre-dominant function.
  - Chromatic mediants are major or minor triads a third away from the home tonic whose roots lie outside the key (bIII, bVI in major; III, VI in minor) — they share a third relation with the tonic but introduce chromaticism, and their parallel chromatic mediant pairs (e.g., C to Ab, both major) produce a striking harmonic jolt used for colour rather than function.
  - Chromatic harmony operates on a spectrum from functional (secondary dominants and augmented sixths that drive toward a cadence) to colouristic (borrowed chords and chromatic mediants that enrich a static loop without forcing resolution), and electronic music overwhelmingly favours the colouristic end.
electronic_music_notes: Chromatic harmony is rarer in electronic music than in jazz or classical because the genre's reliance on loops keeps most tracks in a single key. Borrowed chords are the commonest import and are used for colour rather than function — the bVI and the minor iv appear constantly in house and techno to darken or lift a major-key groove without triggering a real modulation. The bVI–bVII–I cadence is a signature euphoric-house move, the lowered sixth supplying an anthemic lift that the diatonic vi cannot match. Secondary dominants surface in the more harmonically active genres — disco and French house, where the harmonic rhythm is faster — but even there they are usually delivered as single stabs rather than full functional passages. Modulation is uncommon because loops tend to stay in one key, and a true key change inside a looping track can read as awkward or unmotivated. Augmented sixths and chromatic mediants are rare in club music, though the chromatic mediant shift occasionally appears in cinematic or ambient electronic work for dramatic colour. The general rule holds — electronic music borrows chromatic chords for their sound, not for the directional work they do in tonal harmony.
examples:
  - bVI–bVII–I in C major (Ab–Bb–C) — the euphoric-house lift, where the borrowed bVI and bVII from C minor supply an anthemic upward push to the tonic that the diatonic vi–VII–I cannot match.
  - Minor iv in a major-key house track (e.g., Fm resolving to C in C major), a modal-interchange colour that darkens a major groove for a bar before returning to diatonic territory.
  - Secondary dominant V/vi leading to vi (e.g., E7 to Am in C major), where the chromatic G# raised leading tone intensifies the arrival on the relative minor — a move more common in disco and French house than in static-loop genres.
  - Chromatic mediant shift C to Ab (both major triads), a third-apart move outside the key used in ambient or cinematic electronic work for dramatic colour rather than functional direction.

# Chromatic harmony

Chromatic harmony is everything that diatonic harmony is not. Where the diatonic system builds chords only from the home scale, chromatic technique reaches outside it — borrowing chords from the parallel mode, manufacturing dominants for chords that the key does not supply one for, shifting the tonal centre, or inventing sonorities whose voice-leading encodes a chromatic resolution. Each move adds something diatonic harmony cannot: colour that the home scale lacks, or directed pull toward a target that the diatonic chords treat only weakly.

Borrowed chords, or modal interchange, are the gentlest chromatic move. A progression in C major can import the minor-mode iv (Fm), bVI (Ab), or bVII (Bb) from C minor for a bar or two, lending a fleeting minor tinge that resolves back to the major tonic. The chords function as colour, not as a real key change; they widen the palette without forcing a cadence. Secondary dominants work the other end of the spectrum, intensifying function. Any diatonic chord can be treated as a local tonic and given its own dominant — V/V, V/ii, V/vi — so that the arrival on the target chord carries the same raised-leading-tone pull that a real V–I possesses. The chromaticism is in the leading tone: E7 to Am in C major uses G#, a pitch outside the key, to drive the resolution. Modulation changes key outright, either through a pivot chord common to old and new keys, by direct jump, or by holding a common tone across the shift. Augmented sixth chords and chromatic mediants round out the vocabulary — the former chromatic pre-dominants whose voice-leading resolves to V, the latter third-related triads outside the key that produce striking colour shifts.

Electronic music treats this inheritance selectively and leans on the colouristic end. The bVI and minor iv are house and techno staples, darkening or lifting a major groove without triggering a real modulation; the bVI–bVII–I cadence is a signature euphoric-house lift. Secondary dominants appear in disco and French house, where the harmonic rhythm is fast enough to accommodate them, but rarely in static-loop genres. Modulation is uncommon because loops stay in one key, and augmented sixths and chromatic mediants are largely confined to cinematic or ambient work. The general pattern: electronic music borrows chromatic chords for their sound, not for the directional work they do in tonal harmony.

## Sources

- Edward Aldwell & Carl Schachter, *Harmony & Voice Leading*, Harcourt Brace — the construction of secondary dominants as applied V–I relationships, modal interchange as borrowing from the parallel mode, and the voice-leading of augmented sixth chords, summarised in the first two paragraphs in the project's own words.
- Stefan Kostka & Dorothy Payne, *Tonal Harmony*, McGraw-Hill — the classification of chromatic techniques (borrowed chords, secondary dominants, modulation, chromatic mediants) and the augmented sixth chord types (Italian, French, German), restated without verbatim copying.
- Arnold Schoenberg, *Theory of Harmony* (Harmonielehre), University of California Press — the framing of chromatic harmony as a widening of the diatonic palette toward remote regions, paraphrased in the opening paragraph.
- Mark Jamieson, *Electronic Music Production: The Present and Future*; widely documented production practice — the bVI–bVII–I euphoric-house lift, the minor iv as a house and techno colour, the appearance of secondary dominants in disco and French house, and the rarity of modulation and augmented sixths in loop-based club music, drawn from common production knowledge and genre convention.
