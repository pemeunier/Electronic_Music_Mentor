# Bass-as-harmony

Bass-as-harmony is the approach in which the bass line carries the harmonic identity of a track and the chords above it — if there are any — supply colour rather than direction. The premise rests on a simple acoustic fact: the ear hears the lowest sounding pitch as the root of the harmony. Whatever note sits in the bass defines what chord the moment is, so when the bass moves, the bass is what changes the harmony. This is the inverse of chord-led harmony, where triads in the middle register drive the music and the bass follows underneath as support; here the relationship is flipped, and the stabs or pads above become textural colour over a bass doing the harmonic work.

The approach pervades electronic music for structural reasons. The bass is the foundation of the genre's sound — it couples with the kick, fills the sub region, and is what dancers feel — so it is the natural carrier of harmonic information. Loop-based music is bass-driven music: a riff repeats, and the ear hears each repetition as a static harmonic field punctuated by the bass's own movement. Chord stabs, where they appear, are textural events — short hits adding major, minor, or suspended colour above whatever root the bass holds, not vehicles for functional progression. In some genres, most plainly techno, the bass carries the harmony with no chords at all, the identity following the bare bass line — an austerity that suits music designed to hypnotise rather than develop.

The practical discipline is therefore not voice-leading but register management and colour choice. The bass belongs low — the sub root often below 100 Hz, the body up to roughly 200 Hz — and the stabs must sit higher, commonly an octave or more above, so the low-mid does not crowd and the kick keeps its space; failure to separate the two is the commonest cause of a muddy mix. Above each bass note the producer chooses which colour to add — a C bass under a C minor, Ab major, or Gsus stab yields three different emotional readings of the same root — and that choice, repeated or varied as the bass moves, is where the harmonic thinking happens. Wide bass leaps that classical voice-leading would forbid are idiomatic here, because the bass is carrying the harmony, not acting as a voice inside a chord.

## Sources

- Edward Aldwell & Carl Schachter, *Harmony & Voice Leading*, Harcourt Brace — the principle that the bass defines the root and therefore the chord identity, and the contrast with chord-led harmony in which the progression lives in the upper voices, paraphrased in the opening in the project's own words.
- Stefan Kostka & Dorothy Payne, *Tonal Harmony*, McGraw-Hill — the foundational role of the bass in determining chord inversion and identity, restated without verbatim copying as the theoretical basis for bass-as-harmony.
- Mark Jamieson, *Electronic Music Production: The Present and Future*; widely documented production practice — the root-fifth-octave bass idiom, the chord stab as textural colour above a static bass, register separation between bass and stabs, and techno's bare-bass austerity, drawn from common production knowledge and genre convention.
- Rick Snoman, *The Dance Music Manual*, Focal Press — the coupling of bass and kick, sub-bass frequency regions, and the low-mid crowding problem that makes register separation central to electronic production, summarised from standard mixing practice.
