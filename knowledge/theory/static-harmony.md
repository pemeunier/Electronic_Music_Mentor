# Static harmony

Static harmony is harmony that refuses to move — or moves only between two chords — and lets rhythm, texture, and density do the work that progressions do elsewhere. In its purest form it is a pedal point: a single chord, often a minor sonority or a bare root-fifth drone, sustained while everything around it evolves. In a slightly more generous form it oscillates between two chords — i–VII or i–VI in minor — a cell short enough that the ear hears it as a field rather than a journey. The harmonic material is fixed, and interest is generated elsewhere.

This is the opposite of progression-based thinking, where departures from and returns to a tonic supply the narrative. In static harmony there is no departure and no return, only a sustained state. What changes is the surface: filter sweeps open and close on a pad, layers enter and leave, percussion thickens and thins, the register spreads upward or contracts to a single line. Tension is built through density (more elements, more activity), register (a high line brightening over a dark drone), and rhythm (a busier pattern over an unchanged chord), not through harmonic direction. The bass typically holds the pedal — a low root, sometimes a root-fifth ostinato — while the textures above shift; in bass-as-harmony terms, the bass carries the harmonic identity and everything above is colour.

The discipline of static harmony is therefore not harmonic but textural and formal. The producer's craft is sustaining interest without harmonic change — using filter movement, adding and removing layers, evolving percussion, and shifting the textural surface so the track feels like it is going somewhere even though the chords are not. This is a defining feature of techno, dub techno, and minimal electronic, where a single chord or two-chord loop can sustain for minutes and the music hypnotises through accumulation rather than development. Static harmony is not harmony without progress — the progress is textural and rhythmic, and the skill is making that progress audible.
