# Rhythmic subdivision and groove

Rhythmic subdivision is the grid a pattern is built on, and groove is the feel that comes from how the pattern sits against that grid. In most electronic music the grid is the sixteenth note — each beat divided into four steps — because step sequencers and drum machines work that way, and because a sixteenth grid is fine enough to carry syncopation while coarse enough to stay readable. At zero swing the result is metronomic, which is the desired sound in much of techno where the rigidity itself is the groove. Triplets subdivide each beat into three instead of four, giving a rolling feel that underlies shuffle, garage, and certain jungle patterns, and the choice of subdivision is a genre-defining decision made before any hit is placed.

Swing is the most common deviation from the straight grid. It delays the off-beats — in house, the second sixteenth of each beat is pushed late, typically by 55-60%, so the hat pattern lopes rather than marches. The higher the swing, the closer the off-beat moves toward the next triplet, and the looser the feel. The pocket is a related micro-timing concern but applies to the kick: where it sits relative to the beat. Slightly ahead (pushing) feels urgent and driving; slightly behind (pulling) feels laid-back and heavy, and the bass locks to the kick so the two move as one. In electronic music these offsets are dialled in with swing parameters and groove templates rather than played, which makes groove a production craft — the producer sculpts feel with millisecond-scale nudges.

Velocity is groove as much as timing. A gridded pattern with flat velocity sounds dead; the same pattern with dynamic accents comes alive. Hi-hat velocity in particular carries the groove — loud on key steps, ghosted on others — and the interplay of strong and weak hits shapes the feel even when every note is exactly on the grid. Polyrhythm adds a further layer: three against four, or a three-note ostinato over a four-beat bar, creates offset patterns that resolve only across multiple bars. Different genres use these tools as signatures — house swing, garage shuffle, techno's rigid grid, jungle's breakbeat polyrhythm — and the craft is choosing where to lock to the grid and where to loosen, where to push the kick and where to lay it back.
