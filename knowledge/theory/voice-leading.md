# Voice-leading

Voice-leading is the craft of connecting chords so that the individual lines (voices) move smoothly from one harmony to the next. In the common-practice tradition that codified the discipline, the goal is to make each voice sing as if it were an independent melodic strand while still producing the intended vertical sonorities. The central technique is the preservation of common tones: when two adjacent chords share a pitch, that voice stays put, and the remaining voices move by the shortest available interval — usually a step. Where a leap is unavoidable, it is taken in an inner voice so the ear does not register it as a melodic event.

A few hard prohibitions shape classical voice-leading. Parallel fifths and parallel octaves between any two voices are avoided because they cause the two lines to fuse sonically, eroding the independence of parts that the style depends on. The outer voices — soprano and bass — receive the most attention, since the ear tracks them most clearly: the bass defines the harmony and the soprano carries the principal melody. Tendency tones are resolved according to their nature. The leading tone (the seventh scale degree) pulls upward to the tonic; the chord seventh falls by step to the third of the next chord. These resolutions give progressions their sense of direction and inevitability.

Electronic music changes the picture. When the bass line carries the harmony on its own — a defining feature of many house, techno, and bass-music idioms — wide bass leaps are not only acceptable but idiomatic; the bass is the harmony, and the upper parts are texture rather than independent lines. Chord stabs frequently arrive as simultaneous blocks with no attempt at smooth connection between iterations, and the music loops rather than develops, so voice-leading concerns shift from melodic motion to register spacing: avoiding a cluttered low-mid, keeping the sub-bass clear for the kick, and distributing parts across the spectrum. Parallelism, far from being a fault, is often the point — power-chord movement, stacked-fifths pads, and parallel synth riffs are genre-defining sounds rather than voice-leading errors. The discipline remains useful — smooth pads and well-voiced chords still benefit from common-tone thinking — but the rules that govern it are the genre's rules, not the textbook's.

## Sources

- Edward Aldwell & Carl Schachter, *Harmony & Voice Leading*, Harcourt Brace — the classical discipline of common-tone preservation, outer-voice care, and tendency-tone resolution summarized in the first paragraph, synthesized from their treatment of voice-leading in diatonic harmony.
- Arnold Schoenberg, *Theory of Harmony* (Harmonielehre), University of California Press — the framing of voice-leading as the connection of independent lines rather than the mere succession of chords, paraphrased in the opening.
- Stefan Kostka & Dorothy Payne, *Tonal Harmony*, McGraw-Hill — the standard prohibitions on parallel fifths and octaves and the resolution of the leading tone and chord seventh, restated in the project's own words.
- General electronic-music production literature (e.g., Mark Jamieson, *Electronic Music Production: The Present and Future*; widely documented production practice) — the bass-as-harmony idiom, static-loop register spacing, and the acceptance of parallelism as an idiomatic rather than erroneous sound, drawn from common production knowledge and genre convention.
