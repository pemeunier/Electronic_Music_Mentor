# Diatonic harmony

Diatonic harmony is the system that grows naturally out of a single scale. Build a triad on each degree of a major scale and you get a fixed sequence of chord qualities — in C major, C (I), Dm (ii), Em (iii), F (IV), G (V), Am (vi), and B° (vii°). Do the same in a minor scale and you get i–ii°–III–iv–v–VI–VII, with harmonic minor's raised leading tone supplying the V (or vii°) that cadences actually use. Roman numerals name each chord by scale degree and quality, so a progression can be spoken of independent of key: a ii–V–I in C and a ii–V–I in F are the same construct, transposed.

The seven triads sort into three functions. Tonic chords (I or i) are the stable home. Pre-dominant, or subdominant, chords (ii and IV/iv) depart from home and prepare tension. Dominant chords (V and vii°) carry the strongest tension and pull back toward tonic. Most functional progressions are a journey: tonic outward through pre-dominant to dominant, then resolution back. The dominant-to-tonic move — V–I in major, V–i in minor — is the engine of the whole system. Its force comes from the leading tone rising to the tonic and, when the dominant is a seventh chord, the chordal seventh falling by step to the third of the tonic; these directed resolutions give progressions their sense of inevitability. Common progressions compress this journey into reusable shapes: I–V–vi–IV (the "four chords"), I–IV–V, and ii–V–I each state tonic, push out, and return in a different balance.

Electronic music treats this inheritance selectively. House often borrows functional cadences — ii–V–I or I–vi–IV–V delivered as stabs — so the groove inherits tonal direction without ever ceasing to be a groove. Pop-electronic and vocal EDM lean heavily on the four-chord loop, the same I–V–vi–IV that drives mainstream pop. Techno frequently avoids functional harmony entirely, working from a single chord, a drone, or non-functional cells where movement is textural rather than directional. The shift matters conceptually: a two- or three-chord loop repeated indefinitely is not a "progression" in the classical sense, because it does not depart and return so much as establish a static field. Minor keys dominate the darker genres — deep house, techno, drum-and-bass — because the minor mode reads as sombre and tense, and the i–VI–III–VII loop it affords lets harmony hover without the cadential drive of a true dominant.

## Sources

- Edward Aldwell & Carl Schachter, *Harmony & Voice Leading*, Harcourt Brace — the construction of diatonic triads from the scale, their classification by primary function (tonic, pre-dominant, dominant), and the cadential force of V–I, summarised in the first two paragraphs in the project's own words.
- Stefan Kostka & Dorothy Payne, *Tonal Harmony*, McGraw-Hill — the major- and minor-mode diatonic chord qualities, roman-numeral analysis, and standard common progressions (I–IV–V, ii–V–I), restated without verbatim copying.
- Mark Jamieson, *Electronic Music Production: The Present and Future*; widely documented production practice — the house ii–V–I stab idiom, the I–V–vi–IV "four chords" loop in pop-electronic, techno's avoidance of functional harmony, and the dominance of minor-mode i–VI–III–VII loops in deep house and melodic techno, drawn from common production knowledge and genre convention.
