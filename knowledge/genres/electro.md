# Electro

Electro emerged in the early 1980s at the intersection of hip-hop, funk, and electronic music, when producers turned to the Roland TR-808 as a complete rhythm section and built tracks around its deep kick, crisp snare, and cowbell. The lineage runs from Kraftwerk's mechanical funk and Clinton's P-Funk through the Bronx productions that absorbed both, into Detroit and Miami scenes where the 808 became a signature instrument. Across this lineage electro kept a constant identity: the 808 is the band, the vocoder the voice, and the bass the progression. The character was never cosmetic — it came from building funk on machines never meant to swing, and making them swing.

The genre's spirit is a robotic funk. A good electro track feels like a machine that learned to dance. The 808 patterns are syncopated, with kicks and snares off the grid so the groove pushes forward; the 808 bass is melodic and heavy, its pitch movement carrying harmonic changes a progression would elsewhere; the vocoder supplies a cold voice that gives the track identity. The texture is retro-futuristic — 808 drums, vocoder, and cold pads form an era-specific sound. Arrangements move in 16-bar phrases, with vocoder and bass drops as pillars.

Lazy execution keeps the 808 sound but drops the funk. A pattern plays with every hit on the grid and no syncopation, so the groove reads as stiff. A bass line holds the root and never moves, so harmony never changes. A track with no vocoder identity leaves the 808 to carry alone, and cannot. The skill is making the machine move: syncopation in the 808 and a melodic bass that gives coldness a groove.
