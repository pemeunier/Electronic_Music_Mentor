# Jungle / Drum & Bass

Jungle emerged in the UK in the early 1990s from the collision of rave, reggae sound system culture, and hip-hop sampling. Producers sped up breakbeats — most famously the Amen break — and sat them over deep sub-bass lines drawn from Jamaican dub tradition. The tempo climbed into the 160-180 range, the drums fractured into syncopated patterns, and the bass carried the harmonic weight. Through the mid-1990s the music refined itself into drum & bass — cleaner, more produced — while jungle kept the rougher, sample-driven edge. The two terms now name overlapping territory on a continuum: breakbeat-driven, sub-bass-heavy, fast.

The genre's spirit is the coherence of fast complexity. A good track drives on a breakbeat that shifts — ghost snares, kick variations, hat rolls — while a Reese bass moves chromatically underneath and a pad opens the atmosphere. Sub-bass hits below the Reese to give physical impact, and the arrangement builds through snare rolls and rising filters until the drop releases the full breakbeat energy. The drop is the structural pillar; everything else prepares it. The skill is making 170 BPM feel inevitable rather than frantic, holding the breakbeat's intricacy inside a groove.

Lazy execution hits the tempo but loses the internal motion. A breakbeat loops without variation, the Reese sits on one note, and the sub-bass is thin, so the track reads as fast but weightless. The arrangement drifts — drops arrive without preparation, builds stall, energy collapses where it should release. Jungle and DnB without breakbeat variation and bass weight are just fast music; the depth is in the interplay between detailed drums and heavy low end.
