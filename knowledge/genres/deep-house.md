# Deep House

Deep house grew out of 1980s Chicago house, distinguished from its harder, rawer cousins by a deliberate turn toward warmth, soul, and harmonic detail. The "deep" designation came to describe records that drew on jazz, soul, and R&B — chord voicings borrowed from those traditions, vocals carried feeling rather than command, and the groove settled into a comfortable 118-125 BPM band where harmony and texture could breathe. Labels such as Prescription, Cajual, and later Naked Music and Anjunadeep shaped the idiom, but its roots reach back to producers who treated the house groove as a frame for musicality rather than a thing to be amplified.

The genre's spirit is warmth carried by harmonic intention. A good deep house track voices its chords with care — a Rhodes or warm pad stab on a ii-V-I, a bass that walks between the changes rather than pumping a single root, and a swing subtle enough to feel human without announcing itself. The tempo is steady and inviting; the percussion layers a shaker or conga for life rather than density; the reverb sits on the stabs and vocals without smearing the groove. Vinyl crackle, soft saturation, and tape character are not decoration but part of the organic feel that separates "deep" from "digital."

Lazy execution keeps the tempo and the four-on-the-floor kick but loses the soul. A single minor chord stab loops over a rigid, unsprung groove, the bass holds one note, and the mix arrives harsh and digital because nothing was shaped for warmth. Worse, layers pile in — extra percussion, extra pads, extra stabs — until the space that the genre depends on is gone. Deep house is music where each element is chosen and voiced; without that care it is house with a minor mood, not the deep thing itself.
